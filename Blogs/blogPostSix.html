Blog Post - Week #7

Alternative Approaches to Interface Technologies

Thandazile Gcisa
8 April 2021

In the paper of the above tile, authour Steve Gibson examines and explores interfaces that diverge form the standard mouse-keyboard approaches to interacting with digital systems. He suggests that alternative interfaces can generally be described as control schemes that allow the user to connect to a digital environment without the use of conventional mouse-keyboard interfaces.

In the paper, Gibson brings up a few interesting examples to explore alternative approaches. Although there is one particular example that fascinated me the most, which concerns using MIDI instruments as a control scheme. The conventional use of Midi as an interface and language stems from the need for computers and midi instruments to transmit and convey musical information. Essentially midi is a tool for transmitting signal between to subjects and therefore the usefulness of midi is that is not a piano, as it does not transmit audio information between itself and the computer. It only mediates communication between an instrument e.g.) a synthesiser and a computer.

Therefore, the question that arises as Gibson writes is whether midi can be used as an alternative control scheme for non-musical uses. That being said i'd say yes, in fact midi is being used by various individuals as a control scheme for playing games. Considering the fact that this article was written in 2004. Gibson does not make any mention in his examination of games and how they can be controlled by midi instruments.Below is an example of such:


Despite not mentioning games he instead makes reference to visual music. And what he means by this term follows an investigation into the use of midi as an input device for contolling visual environments. As such considering that games are hypermedia a corelation can be made between what he writes about visual music and games. Because his goal was to investigate if "midi can seamlessly control a visual environment to the point where a user would be able to "play" the images in real time as if he or she were "playing" musical notes.

As a result from this example we can deduce that for a midi control scheme to be successful as in the case of games. Users must clearly understand what is happening in the process (immediate feedback) and they must also understand the connection between the 'musical performance' amd the corresponding visual or mechanical result of the game or any other interactive system.
