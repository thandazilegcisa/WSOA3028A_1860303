Digital inequalities of artificial intelligence and big data (Christoph Lutz)

Thandazile Gcisa
20 May 2021

In the article of the above title, authour Christoph Lutz investigates the disparities in the structure of access to and use of ICT as well as how historical socio-economic inequalities shape the expectations and impact of what ICT can provide for life on earth. According to Lutz (2015) traditionally disadvantaged citizens, which we mostly see in the global south are similarly disadvantaged on the internet.Therefore according to Lutz (2015) "optimistic claims that the internet would create widespread social mobility and would lead to less stratified societies have been challenged by digital inequality scholars."

In his article Lutz (2015) defines three levels of the digital divide between the global south and north. He describes the first level as the unequal access to the internet. Which as stated before is a direct reflection of unequal access to economic participation in globalization. He uses the term Digital divide to describe those who do and those who not have access to new forms of information technologies. In the paper he highlights the fact that in most developed countries the first point of entry for users on the internet is computers as compared to mobile in the developing or under-developed global south. He references a proposition made by other scholars that mobile internet access us second-class internet access, and suggests that mobile internet offers lower functionality in terms of speed, memory and storage capacity.

Despite not being completely wrong, he is not completely right either because if accessibility is the core proponent of his first level of digital divide mobile has proven to be considerably more accessibile and has aided penetration of users across the global south without the need to leave out information or enable user-friendly experience. This site is a good example of statement because it takes into consideration the ability of the site to adapt to different resolutions. To allow the site to reflow the content of the page to match different screens, whether rendered on a small mobile phone or a large desktop monitor without actually loosing any content, so i disagree with the authour on that part. Although, i agree that possibly the limitation is that it does not allow the user to contribute to the knowledge base of the internet as argued in the previous article on this site.

Which is the second level that Lutz (2019) implicitly refers to, although he does not talk about the concept in the same vain as Graham et al. (2015) talks about participation. Instead he refers to individuals online recreational activities out site of actually creating on the net. So his argument is not well substantiated as Graham et al. (2015) despite implicitly referring to the same thin "second-digital divide studies have focused on online participation and social media, differentiating active content creators from passive consumers." Lastly he states that the third level divides in such a way that "individuals do not have the capacity to translate their internet access and use into favourable offline outcomes". Lutz goes on to state that studies on third-level digital divide investigate tangible offline outcomes from internet use in economic, social, political and cultural terms. I have to disagree with Lutz again on this point because the very "lowly internet mobile" he describes in the first level is largely responsible for such tangible outcomes. For example M-Pesa, africa's leading payment method is a platform that allows for the transaction of money across africa which is a favourable offline outcome because it means that trade, or deposits to family has become easier to perform so while mobile still provides some limitations in terms of particpation on creating technologies it has somewhat closed the gap in service industries like banking and transportation etc. Therefore, despite being written in 2019 Lutz assumptions and arguments are indicative of the fact that he himself is divided from the south and his paper is not well researched regarding the intricacies of the digital divide as compared to Graham et al. (2015)
