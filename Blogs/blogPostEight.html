Semiotic Design and Analysis of Hypermedia

Thandazile Gcisa
8 April 2021

Monitoring the semiotic design of hypermedia is important mainly because understanding how designers construct meaning through hypermedia is valuable for the designer themselves so that there is semantic coherence across the platform and the contents that it provides. Semiotics as a theory is a very old but gold theory that defines semiotics as signs, signifier that represent or communicate an idea. The very text that you are reading is a referral to semiotics.

Although semiotics has been largely used to analyse film and static artefacts. Currently, the model has and can be used fo design tasks such as developing hypermedia. It attempts to guide the artist or designer in optimising the process if inscribing meaning into the desired system. In their article Toppano and Roberto propose three ways of either analysing (decoding) or inscribing (encoding) semiotic devices to create meaning in hypermedia. They include a narrative approach, a semi-structuralist model, and communication perspectives.

The narrative approach implies that every semiotic device be it drawings, photographs, games or music all have a narrative. And a semi-structuralist model which outlines a continuum of levels that meaning passes through to be construct, the most important (at least to me) being level three, which he defines as a discursive level. This is a level in which "abstract narrative components such as props, set dressing are translated and infer to concrete and thematic elements". Lastly, the communication perspective attempts to assist in analysing hypermedia within a social an cultural context.
This way, the dialogue between the designer or artist and user is mediated by semiotic devices and is inscribed within the text therefore, this substantiate the point that my first blog post makes which is that the medium is the message.
